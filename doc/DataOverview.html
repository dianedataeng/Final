Emergency Room Utilization: Admissions, Costs, and Testing Patterns

Author

Diane Kim

Introduction

Emergency room care is one of the most expensive components of the U.S. healthcare system, and costs have continued to rise. Understanding what drives high ER expenditures and which factors influence inpatient admissions can help policymakers, hospitals, and insurers design more efficient care pathways while reducing patient burden.

Dataset Overview

This analysis uses data from the 2023 Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS). MEPS surveys a geographically diverse sample of U.S. households, then contacts their medical providers—including hospitals, physicians, and pharmacies—to obtain detailed information about healthcare utilization and costs.

The dataset used for this project includes only respondents who reported at least one emergency room visit in 2023.

Data Description

The original MEPS ER visit file includes 4,241 unique patients and 53 variables. According to MEPS, this file captures information about each emergency room visit, including:

  • services and procedures performed,
  • diagnostic testing,
  • medications provided,
  • total expenditures, and
  • sources of payment.

Data Source and Preprocessing

From the original 53 variables, I selected 23 variables relevant to the research questions. Several diagnostic test variables included codes such as −8 (“don’t know”), −7 (“refused”), or 95 (“not received”). All of these were recoded to 0, with “yes” coded as 1, to create consistent binary variables.

Although multiple expenditure variables were available, many represented overlapping cost components. To avoid multicollinearity, only total_cost was used in modeling.

Insurance Variable

To determine insurance status, I reviewed Medicaid, Medicare, and private insurance payments.
- If all insurance payments were zero or missing → uninsured (0)
- If any insurer paid > 0 → insured (1)

Added Demographics

To enrich the analysis, I merged the ER file with the 2023 MEPS Full-Year Consolidated Data using the patient ID. This added:

  • Sex
  • Poverty category (poor/negative → high income)

Research Questions

  1. What predictors help determine whether an ER visit results in an inpatient admission?
  2. Which emergency room visits are associated with the highest total expenditures?
  3. Do uninsured patients receive fewer tests than insured patients? Are there differences in out-of-pocket costs?

Research Question 1: Predicting Inpatient Admission

This question examines which factors contribute to a patient being admitted after an ER visit. The dependent variable was admitted vs. not admitted. Predictor variables included diagnostic tests, procedures, prescriptions, and insurance status.

Objectives

Understanding what drives inpatient admissions can help hospitals anticipate resource needs and assist clinicians in identifying patients who may require extended care.

Data Exploration

A review of the dataset shows: - 21% of patients were admitted
- 79% were not admitted
- MRI/CT usage was more common among admitted patients (35%) than non-admitted (20.5%)

Methodology

Two models were used: - Logistic Regression - Random Forest Classification

Multicollinearity was assessed using VIF. Data were split using createDataPartition() to preserve class imbalance.

Logistic Regression Findings

Significant predictors of higher admission odds included: - lab tests
- related condition
- EKG
- surgery
- ultrasound
- MRI/CT

Insurance status and prescriptions were linked to lower odds of admission.

The model accuracy was ~82%.

Random Forest Classification

The Random Forest achieved similar accuracy (~82%).
Top predictors included: - insurance status
- lab tests
- prescriptions
- EKG

Vaccinations and mammograms had minimal influence.

Best Model

Both models performed similarly, but Random Forest handled class imbalance better and captured non-linear patterns, making it the stronger model.

Research Question 2: Which ER Visits Have the Highest Total Costs?

This section explores what drives high ER spending.

Objectives

Understanding cost drivers can help hospitals manage resource allocation and support efforts to reduce unnecessary spending.

Data Exploration

Total ER costs were highly skewed, with extreme outliers reaching over $100,000.

Median cost: $578
Mean cost: $1,227
Maximum: $195,314

Methodology

Models used: - Random Forest Regression - Multiple Linear Regression

Random Forest Regression

Random Forest was selected because it handles skewed data and outliers well.

Strongest predictors of high costs: - inpatient admission
- surgery

Multiple Linear Regression

Linear regression struggled with outliers.
Significant predictors: - admitted (large negative coefficient)
- MRI/CT (+$936)
- ultrasound (+$648)
- lab tests (+$336)

Best Model

Random Forest was the most reliable model:

Model RMSE MAE R²
Random Forest 997.89 243.08 0.97
Multiple Linear Regression 3689.77 1147.58 0.132

The Random Forest captured a much larger share of cost variation and handled outliers far better.

Research Question 3: What Predicts the Number of Tests a Patient Receives?

This question examines testing variation and potential disparities.

Objectives

The goal is to identify social or financial factors that may influence how many tests or procedures a patient receives during an ER visit.

Data Preparation

A new variable, total_tests, was created by summing all test/procedure indicators.
After merging demographic data, the modeling variables included: - insurance status
- poverty category
- sex

Data Exploration

The dataset included: - 644 uninsured
- 3,597 insured

Most patients received around two tests, regardless of insurance status.

Poverty analysis showed: - uninsured patients most commonly fell into middle- and high-income groups
- near-poor and low-income patients were more often insured

Methodology

Models used: - Multiple Linear Regression - Poisson Regression - Random Forest Regression

Linear Regression Findings

Significant predictors: - high-income patients received 0.24 more tests
- insured patients received 0.17 fewer tests

Poisson Regression

Similar direction of results, with slightly smaller effect sizes.

Random Forest Regression

Most influential predictors: - poverty category
- insurance status
- sex (smallest impact)

Conclusion

Across all three research questions, Random Forest models consistently performed well due to their ability to handle skewed data, outliers, and variable interactions. The analysis highlights strong predictors of admission, key drivers of high ER spending, and socioeconomic factors associated with diagnostic testing patterns.
